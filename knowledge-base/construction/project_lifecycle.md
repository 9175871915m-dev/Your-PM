# Construction Project Lifecycle

## Overview
A construction project typically progresses through five defined phases, each with distinct deliverables, decision gates, and governance requirements.

## Phase 1 – Initiation
- Establish project need and business case
- Appoint Project Sponsor and Project Manager
- Conduct feasibility study and site investigation
- Develop Project Brief (scope, budget, programme outline)
- Complete stakeholder identification
- Key output: **Project Charter / Project Brief**

## Phase 2 – Pre-Construction (Design & Procurement)
- Appoint design team (Architect, Civil/Structural, MEP engineers)
- Develop RIBA Stages 1–4 (Preparation, Concept, Spatial, Technical)
- Conduct planning application and obtain statutory consents
- Complete tender documents and procurement strategy
- Evaluate and appoint main contractor
- Key frameworks: **NEC3/NEC4, FIDIC, JCT, CDM 2015**
- Key output: **Signed Contract, Execution Plan, Construction Programme**

## Phase 3 – Construction (Execution)
- Establish site setup and works commencement
- Monitor progress against baseline programme (typically P6 or MS Project)
- Manage Requests for Information (RFIs), Submittals, and Variation Orders
- Conduct weekly Site Progress Meetings (SPM) with minutes
- Monitor cost against Earned Value Management (EVM) metrics
- Apply CDM 2015 — Principal Contractor duties
- Key output: **Weekly Progress Reports, Updated Programme, Cost Reports**

## Phase 4 – Commissioning & Testing
- System-by-system functional testing (mechanical, electrical, structural)
- Snagging list (Defects List) preparation
- Practical Completion inspections
- O&M Manuals and as-built drawings handover
- Key output: **Certificate of Practical Completion (CPC)**

## Phase 5 – Close-Out & Handover
- Final account settlement (application for payment, final certificate)
- Defects Liability Period (DLP) monitoring (typically 12 months)
- Lessons Learned workshops
- Archive all project documentation
- Key output: **Final Completion Certificate, Lessons Learned Register**

## Key Success Factors
1. Clear scope definition at Initiation — scope creep is the #1 cause of overruns
2. Robust change control from Day 1
3. Regular programme updates — minimum monthly re-baseline
4. Early contractor involvement (ECI) reduces risk
5. Proactive stakeholder communication
